The Unwritten Rules of Run Club

Nobody hands you a manual on your first day. Here's what we wish someone had told us.

You never really know why someone shows up to their first group run. Some people are chasing a fitness goal. Some just moved to town and don't know a soul yet. Some want to be more social, or they're testing the waters to see what a "run club" actually is before they commit to anything. And some are just lonely, and a group run is a good enough reason to leave the house on a Saturday morning.

Whatever brought someone through the door, they're probably nervous. That matters more than their pace does.

If you're welcoming someone new

The best thing you can do for a first-timer isn't a pep talk, it's just noticing them. Say hi before they have to introduce themselves. Ask what brought them out, and actually listen to the answer, because it tells you what they need from the group. Someone who just moved to town wants friends. Someone who's nervous about their fitness wants to not feel judged. Someone who's there to be social wants a conversation, not a training plan.

If you're the one who's new

Follow the lead. If the group says it's a 30-minute run, run 30 minutes. This isn't the day to prove anything to anyone, including yourself. The people leading the run are almost certainly still keeping an eye on you, still figuring out your pace, still quietly making sure you're okay and not getting dropped. Let them do that job. There will be plenty of runs later to push yourself. Your first one isn't the time.

Groups find their people

In a small club, or one that's just getting off the ground, you might end up as your own built-in friend group simply because you're some of the only regulars there. In a bigger club, something different happens...people naturally drift toward whoever they click with. That's not cliquish, it's just how groups of humans work. If it happens to you as the new person, it isn't personal. Give it time, and you'll find your people too.

Pace is personal, not a pecking order

At some point you'll notice one of the faster runners hanging back on an easy day, and it's tempting to read something into it. Are they injured, off their game, having a bad week? Probably none of that. Sometimes a fast runner just wants a slow day. Sometimes they want to run with someone specific. Sometimes they just don't feel like pushing. Pace on any given day says very little about someone's fitness or commitment. It's personal, and it changes.

Some people linger, some people bolt

After the run, some people want to stand around for another half hour talking. Others have to get straight to the car — a job to get to, kids to pick up, a life outside the club waiting on them. Neither one is rude. Read the room, and don't take it personally either way.

If there's one unwritten rule underneath all the others, it's this: everyone showed up for a different reason. The club works best when people leave each other room for that.
